The Absurdity, in Numbers

Some things that have happened in the time it's taken to not fix a bridge

£250,000,000

The Ever-Rising Quote

The repair estimate has nearly doubled from £141M in 2020 to £250M today. At this rate of increase, it'll cost £500M by the time they actually start.

£54M+ spent

Spent With No Cars Crossing

Over £54 million of public money has been spent — primarily on "stabilisation" — and not a single motor vehicle has crossed the bridge since. That's roughly £22,000 per day spent on a bridge cars can't use.

#1 Most Congested Road

Putney Pays the Price

In 2022, the INRIX Global Traffic Scorecard ranked the A219 through Putney as London's most congested road — in Europe's most congested city. Drivers lost 47 hours a year on that stretch alone. INRIX explicitly cited the Hammersmith Bridge closure as a contributing factor.

5 Bus Routes

Diverted Into Oblivion

Routes 33, 72, 209, 419, and 485 have been affected. Thousands of daily commuters have had their journeys extended, some permanently rerouted through already congested corridors.

"Monument" Proposal

Yes, Really

In January 2025, the Department for Transport genuinely suggested turning the bridge into an inaccessible "monument" — or demolishing it entirely. Both proposals were rejected. The fact they were made at all tells you everything.

4 Years to Drill Test Holes

The Foster Plan, Killed by Delay

Foster + Partners designed a solution that would have had cars crossing by autumn 2021. It needed pier drilling to confirm feasibility. That drilling was agreed in late 2020, started years later, completed in July 2024 — and then both the DfT and council refused to publish the results.

3 IRA Bombs Survived

But Not Bureaucracy

Hammersmith Bridge survived IRA bomb attacks in 1939, 1996, and 2000. It turns out the one thing that can destroy a Victorian bridge is a funding dispute between three public bodies.

The Full Timeline of Inaction

A decade of warnings, closures, proposals, rejections, and finger-pointing

2006 – 2014

Maintenance Budget Slashed

The council's annual bridge maintenance budget was cut from £1 million to £250,000. The money that was spent went on cosmetic works like wood panel refurbishment — not structural integrity.

H&F Council
2014

First Alarm Bells

A structural report raises serious concerns about the bridge's resilience. The newly elected council commissions the first Comprehensive Structural Integrity Review in the bridge's history — 127 years after it opened.

Engineers / Hayder
November 2016

Funding Wrangle Begins

Repairs and strengthening works are delayed as H&F Council and TfL can't agree who should pay. The bridge continues carrying 20,000 vehicles daily with known structural concerns.

H&F Council TfL
10 April 2019

Bridge Closed to Motor Vehicles

The bridge is closed indefinitely to all motor traffic due to cracks in the iron pedestals. 20,000 daily vehicle journeys are displaced overnight. Pedestrians and cyclists can still cross. The closure was meant to be temporary.

H&F Council
August 2019

Mayor Asks for a Meeting

The Mayor of London raises the bridge closure with the Transport Secretary and requests a meeting. The wheels of bureaucracy turn at their customary pace.

Mayor / TfL
September 2019

TfL Pledges £25M

Feasibility studies are completed. TfL confirms its contribution of £25 million. This sounds like progress. It isn't — nobody else has confirmed their share.

TfL
13 August 2020

Total Closure — Heatwave Damage

A heatwave causes the structural issues to worsen dramatically. The bridge is now closed to everyone: pedestrians, cyclists, river traffic — even boats can't pass underneath. Full repair estimate: £141M. Stabilisation alone: £46M. Nobody has the money.

Engineers
October 2020

£16.7M Spent on Plans Alone

It's revealed that TfL has spent £16.7 million just on repair planning. Not on repairs — on planning for repairs that haven't happened.

TfL
November 2020

Foster + Partners Proposes Bold Solution

After the full closure, Council leader Stephen Cowan reaches out to property tycoon and philanthropist Sir John Ritblat for help. Ritblat approaches Foster + Partners, who team up with specialist bridge engineers COWI. Their proposal: a temporary double-decker steel truss built above the existing deck — a bridge-within-a-bridge. The lower level carries pedestrians and cyclists; the upper level carries cars and buses. The structure wouldn't rest on the historic suspension system, so workers could safely access and even remove damaged elements off-site for repair. Assembled on both banks and slid into place to meet in the middle. Cost: ~£100M — a potential £40M saving over the conventional £141M approach. Pedestrians by summer 2021, cars by autumn 2021, full restoration by 2023. H&F Council immediately backs it. But the council doesn't have £100M — and crucially, it doesn't own the decision alone. TfL and the DfT both need to agree and contribute funding. Neither commits. What follows is a masterclass in how three public bodies can collectively kill a good idea without any one of them formally rejecting it.

Foster + Partners / COWI H&F Council
December 2020

But First: Can the Piers Take the Weight?

The proposal has one critical dependency: can the bridge's pedestal footings support the additional mass of a double-decker structure? Nobody knows — no risk assessment has been done. Foster/COWI recommend drilling into the piers to test load-bearing capacity, a standard investigative step that should take months. Without this data, no contractor will take on the project. The drilling is agreed in principle at a DfT taskforce meeting. It will not be completed for nearly four years.

Foster + Partners / COWI DfT Taskforce
March 2021

Toll Scheme Agreed — Then Stalls

A government taskforce agrees to a plan where a £3 toll would fund the council's share of the Foster scheme. The plan is "accepted." But "accepted" apparently doesn't mean "funded." Nobody signs the cheque. The DfT won't commit money. TfL, financially crippled by the pandemic, can't contribute its pledged £25M. No contractor will take on the Foster scheme's technical risk without the pier drilling data — which everyone agreed to collect but nobody is collecting. The plan dies the most British death imaginable: everyone agrees it's a good idea, and nobody does anything about it.

Government Taskforce TfL
17 July 2021

Reopens to Pedestrians (But Dismount Your Bike)

After 11 months of total closure, the bridge reopens to pedestrians. Cyclists can cross — but must push their bikes. This "dismount" rule will persist for nearly three years.

H&F Council
February 2022

Phase 1 Stabilisation Begins

Engineers from Mott MacDonald begin on-site stabilisation work. The goal is to keep the bridge from deteriorating further while the adults in the room figure out who pays for the rest.

Mott MacDonald
December 2022

Business Case Submitted — Then Ignored

H&F Council submits a plan to the Department for Transport for full reopening, including a toll for drivers. The DfT takes no action. A formal resubmission in March 2023 also receives no response.

Department for Transport H&F Council
July 2023

DfT Says Bridge "Not Eligible" for Funding

The Department for Transport confirms that Hammersmith Bridge — a Grade II* listed national monument — is not eligible for many sources of infrastructure funding. You couldn't make it up.

Department for Transport
April 2024 — 5th Anniversary

"Could Take Another Five Years"

On the fifth anniversary of closure, the council warns it could take another five years to reopen to vehicles, even if full funding appeared immediately. The cost estimate has risen to £250M. Around £30M has already been spent.

H&F Council
July 2024

Pier Drilling Finally Completed — 4 Years Late

Remember the "standard investigative step" of drilling into the piers — the one thing standing between the Foster plan and a contractor signing up? It was proposed in late 2020. It is now July 2024. The council finally completes the drilling — nearly four years after it was first agreed. Four years. To drill some test holes. In that time, the UK built and opened the Elizabeth Line. By now, the Foster plan's original £100M estimate is long obsolete. The cost of any full restoration has ballooned to £250M. The window where the plan could have had cars crossing by autumn 2021 is a distant memory.

H&F Council
Late 2024

FOI Requests Refused — Results Kept Secret

After the pier drilling is completed, both the DfT and H&F Council refuse Freedom of Information requests to disclose the results. The public — who funded the drilling — are not allowed to know whether the Foster plan is structurally feasible. The data that could have unlocked progress sits behind a bureaucratic wall. The plan that would have had cars crossing by autumn 2021 is effectively buried, not by engineering failure, but by administrative suffocation.

DfT H&F Council
January 2025

Government Suggests Demolishing It

At the reconvened Hammersmith Bridge Taskforce, the DfT floats two extraordinary ideas: turn the bridge into an inaccessible "monument," or demolish it and build a new crossing. Both are rejected. Cars being banned permanently is also discussed as a cost-saving measure.

Department for Transport
April 2025

New Cycle & Pedestrian Lanes Open

The main carriageway is resurfaced and reopened with dedicated cycling and pedestrian lanes — a £2.9M project. A genuine improvement for non-motorised users. Cars? Still banned.

H&F Council
June 2025

Government Announces "Structures Fund"

Ministers announce a new pot of money — the Structures Fund — set aside to support "repairing run down bridges, decaying flyovers and worn out tunnels across Britain." It sounds promising. But there is no specific commitment to Hammersmith Bridge yet.

Department for Transport
February 2026 LATEST

A Glimmer? Taskforce Reconvened, Fund Earmarked

Roads Minister Simon Lightwood announces the DfT will reconvene the Hammersmith Bridge Taskforce — and specifically identifies the bridge as "a good candidate for investment" from the Structures Fund. He states the government intends to "consider the viability of future funding for the next stage of works through this route." Note the language: consider the viability of future funding. Not "fund." Not "commit." Consider. The viability. Of future funding. As the bridge approaches its 7th anniversary of closure to cars on 10 April 2026.

Where things stand: £54M+ of public money spent. £20.2M owed to the council by DfT and TfL. Repair estimate: £250M. No reopening date for motor vehicles. The saga enters its eighth year.

£16.7M on "Planning"

TfL's Invisible Spending

On top of the £2.9M contributed to actual work, TfL spent £16.7M on assessments, feasibility studies, inspectors, surveys and designs. That's £16.7 million on plans for repairs that never happened. For context, £16.7M is enough to build a new primary school.

£20.2M Owed

The Council's Unpaid Bill

H&F Council says it is owed £20.2M by the DfT and TfL — money the council advanced in good faith, often without government approval, because somebody had to keep the bridge from collapsing while the adults argued about whose chequebook to use.

£0 from TfL's 2024 Plan

Dropped Entirely

Despite pledging £25M in 2019, TfL's 2024 Business Plan did not include any funding for Hammersmith Bridge. Not reduced funding. Not deferred funding. No funding at all. The bridge was simply dropped from the plan as though it doesn't exist.

Who Is Blocking What?

Three public bodies, one bridge, zero accountability

Hammersmith & Fulham Council

Bridge owner & maintainer
Cut maintenance budget from £1M to £250K (2006–14), which contributed to the bridge's deterioration. But since 2020, has spent £43M+ — 80% of all money spent on the bridge — while the other two parties dragged their feet. Backed the Foster + Partners plan. The council isn't blameless, but it's the only body that has actually put its money where its mouth is.

Department for Transport ⚠

Central government — should be funding this
The DfT routinely funds 80–85% of bridge projects across the UK. It paid 85% of the Tyne Bridge restoration (also Grade II* listed). It paid 82% of the Great Yarmouth crossing. It committed £3.1 billion to the Lower Thames Crossing before construction has even started. Yet for Hammersmith Bridge, it has contributed just £8.3M — 15% of spend — and declared the bridge "not eligible" for infrastructure funding. When pressed, it suggested demolishing a Grade II* national monument or turning it into a "landmark" that nobody can cross. This is a total failure of the DfT's obligation to fund national infrastructure.

Transport for London ⚠

London's transport authority — pledged £25M, delivered £2.9M
Pledged £25M in 2019 and has delivered just £2.9M — 88% short of its own promise. Separately burnt £16.7M on planning that led nowhere. Meanwhile, TfL committed £2.2 billion to the Silvertown Tunnel in east London — paying £100M per year for 25 years. It found £2.2B for a new tunnel, but can't honour a £25M pledge to repair an existing bridge serving a quarter of a million people? TfL then dropped Hammersmith Bridge from its 2024 Business Plan entirely. Not reduced. Not deferred. Deleted. As if the bridge doesn't exist.

Historic England / Listed Status

Heritage protection
The Grade II* listing adds complexity and cost to every proposed repair. But the Tyne Bridge is also Grade II* listed and is being restored with 85% government funding. Heritage status is a challenge, not an excuse — it hasn't stopped any other bridge project in the country.

What If We Applied This Logic Elsewhere?

Imagine if other London bridges got the Hammersmith treatment

Putney Bridge Closes Next

Putney already absorbs much of the displaced traffic. If it closed, that's another 37,000 daily vehicle trips diverted. Wandsworth and Battersea Bridge would collapse under the load. South-west London would functionally disconnect from the north.

Chiswick Bridge Goes Down

The A316 corridor — already strained — loses its last easy crossing west of Putney. Kew Bridge becomes the only option. Richmond, Mortlake, and Chiswick grind to a standstill. The M4 becomes the de facto river crossing.

The "Monument" Approach for Tower Bridge

Using the DfT's own logic: Tower Bridge is even older and more historically significant. Should we close it to traffic and make it an "inaccessible monument" too? Of course not. The idea is absurd — which is exactly the point.

The NHS Analogy

Imagine a hospital closing its A&E department in 2019 due to cracked walls, and in 2026 the government is still debating whether to fix it, demolish it, or turn it into a museum. That's where we are with Hammersmith Bridge.

For Context

Things that have been started and finished in the time it's taken to not fix one bridge

The Elizabeth Line

42km of New Tunnels Under London

Crossrail broke ground in 2009 and opened in 2022 — delivering 42km of new railway, 10 new stations, and capacity for 200 million passengers a year. It was considered scandalously late. Hammersmith Bridge has been closed longer than it took to build and open the Elizabeth Line.

Wembley Stadium

90,000 Seats in 4.5 Years

The old Wembley was demolished and an entirely new 90,000-seat national stadium was designed, built and opened between 2002 and 2007. In the time Hammersmith Bridge has been closed to cars, you could have rebuilt Wembley one and a half times.

2–3 Hospitals

What £250M Could Build Instead

The government's New Hospital Programme averages ~£93M per hospital. The £250M repair estimate for Hammersmith Bridge could build two to three entire new hospitals. The £54M already spent on stabilisation alone could have built a new mental health facility.

£40M → £250M

The Cost of Delay Is Delay

When the bridge first closed in 2019, the repair estimate was £40M. By 2020 it was £141M. By 2024 it was £250M. Every year of dithering doesn't just cost money — it makes the final bill bigger. At this rate, the estimate will hit £500M before a decision is made. Inaction is the most expensive option of all.

How We Estimated These Numbers

Days closed: Calculated from 10 April 2019 (motor vehicle closure date) to today. The bridge was additionally closed to all users from 13 August 2020 to 17 July 2021.

Extra vehicle-hours: Before closure, ~20,000 vehicles/day used the bridge. Studies indicate ~15,000 of those rerouted (the rest were "evaporated" — trips not taken or shifted to other modes). We estimate an average of 12 minutes additional journey time per diverted vehicle (conservative, given Putney Bridge queues), yielding ~3,000 extra vehicle-hours per weekday. Summed over ~1,750 weekdays since closure (with reduced estimates for weekends and pandemic periods), this produces our running total.

CO₂ emissions: Using the 15,000 diverted vehicles figure, an average additional distance of 3.2km per diversion, and DEFRA's average car emission factor of 164g CO₂/km, we estimate additional daily emissions of ~7.9 tonnes CO₂. This is cumulated over the closure period with adjustments for pandemic-era traffic reductions (2020–21).

Money spent: Based on H&F Council's publicly reported figure of £54M+ spent by October 2024, which includes £37M on stabilisation and £17M on pre-restoration works. We extrapolate ongoing costs at approximately £15,000/day based on reported spending rates.

Traffic data: DfT traffic counts show Putney Bridge vehicle numbers rose 16% from 32,083 to 37,239 between 2020–2023. The INRIX 2022 Global Traffic Scorecard ranked the A219 through Putney High Street as London's most congested corridor, with drivers losing 47 hours per year — and explicitly cited the Hammersmith Bridge closure as a contributing factor. However, some analyses suggest overall area-wide traffic fell due to pandemic effects and trip evaporation. We present both perspectives.

A note on honesty: Some numbers on this site are estimates. Where data is solid, we say so. Where we're extrapolating, we say that too. The point isn't spurious precision — it's to make tangible what years of inaction actually cost. All source data comes from council reports, DfT statistics, TfL data, and parliamentary records.
